Abstract

The poster discusses energy needs in Egypt and global challenges related to climate change. It presents a solution using a Savonius Vertical-axis Wind Turbine (VAWT) with pumped hydro storage (PHS) for energy through hydraulic head and water flow.

Energy stands at the heart of both global development and environmental crisis whether derived from renewable or non-renewable sources. Introduction

Energy stands at the heart of both global development and environmental crisis whether derived from renewable or non-renewable sources. In the primary component of our project, we designed a Savonius Vertical-axis Wind Turbine (VAWT) with pumped hydro storage (PHS) that offers a practical solution by generating clean energy through hydraulic head and water flow.

According to Egypt's Vision 2030, 42% of the national energy supply must come from renewable by 2035, aiming to bridge energy future and present sustainability.

Materials & Methods

Wind Turbine Construction

The wind turbine was constructed using a PVC pipe and four printed blades. First, a 15 cm-long plastic tube (3.5 cm diameter) was partly flattened on one end and a metal rod inserted and fastened with hot glue. The tube was then attached to a wooden base with a hole in the center, achieving a snug fit without the use of additional couplings.

Next, four 3D-printed blades were attached to the tube using hot glue. The blades were designed to catch the wind and convert it to rotational energy. Finally, the Savonius-blade assembly was mounted on a short steel stand.

Pumped Hydro Storage (PHS) Setup

The PHS system was designed to pump water to a higher elevation, storing energy in the form of potential energy. The system consists of a water pump connected via a short length of flexible hose to a micro-turbine turbine. The pump is powered by the energy generated from the wind turbine.

When discharge is needed, the ball valve is opened and water flows through the micro-turbine, converting the stored potential energy back into electrical energy.

Test Plan

The testing methodology involved measuring the electrical output of the turbine and its ability to supply enough energy to run the water pump. The Savonius's performance was evaluated under standard speed ranges from 4 m/s to 7 m/s.

For the hydro test, four tests were conducted by varying the water pump's position relative to the water surface. Additionally, the electrical output of the micro-turbine turbine was measured during water discharge.

Key Findings

  • The wind turbine produced sufficient energy to power the water pump at wind speeds above 5 m/s
  • The hydro storage system successfully stored and released energy with measurable electrical output
  • Higher water column heights resulted in greater power generation during discharge
  • The system demonstrated the viability of the combined wind-hydro energy storage concept

Analysis

The increase in the use of renewable energy sources is becoming more common problems of energy in Egypt. This decision has led to serious environmental issues, such as water and air pollution. This project aims to provide a sustainable solution to energy problems in Egypt, aiming to promote sustainable energy production.

The prototype was tested for energy output and durability by some steps:

  • Wind turbine performance was measured at different wind speeds
  • The water pumping capacity was evaluated at various power inputs
  • Energy storage efficiency was calculated based on input/output ratios
  • The micro-turbine output was measured during controlled discharge tests

Conclusions

After analyzing the data and testing the prototype, various conclusions were determined regarding energy output and efficiency levels. In the energy output test, the wind turbine successfully generated electrical power that was sufficient to operate the water pump, confirming the compatibility of the design, with a total system volume of only 0.0027 m³. In addition, during the pumped hydro system, the prototype successfully fulfilled its energy generation targets and other design requirements, including sustainability, portability, low maintenance costs, and low operational costs.

Recommendations

After converting the prototype, recommendations were made based on experimental findings to enhance the system's performance. Specifically, using larger impeller sizes and more segments in the turbine blades is recommended for better energy output. Additionally, the system should be tested in real wind conditions in Egypt, due to its steady wind availability and natural access to national energy grids, making it an ideal location for renewable energy development.
